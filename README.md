# A "Relationships" Framework: Theory

---

## Background Information

### Goals

This framework describes the conditions under which genuine bonds form between minds. It is
built to operate outside of two framings that have distorted most existing relationship research
and advice.

**Amatonormativity** holds that the romantic dyad is the highest and most legitimate form of
human bond — that all other connections are lesser approximations of it. Under amatonormativity,
friendship is romance without sex; chosen family is substitution for a "real" family; sexual
partnerships outside romantic commitment are treated as failures of self-control. This framework
does not accept that hierarchy. The romantic couple is one instance of a bond, given no special
status.

**Kinship normativity** holds that blood and legal family are the primary valid bonds, and that the
goal of life is reproductive family formation. This framework does not accept that assumption
either. A polycule, a friend group, a chosen family, a creative collective — these are as legitimate
as any biological family, and this framework is built for all of them from the start.

The framework is therefore built for:

- **Any number of participants.** Dyads, triads, polycules, friend groups, chosen families,
  collectives. These are not modifications of a dyadic default. Two people is one case, not the
  standard case.

- **Any mind.** The framework does not assume a standard neurotype or a sanity baseline. It is
  built for the full range of minds that meet the requirements defined below. Why the "standard
  mind" is a fiction is explained in the section that follows.

- **Any form of shared goal.** Profit, rebellion, emancipation, sex, reproduction, domestic life,
  entertainment, creative work, or no goal at all — a bond for its own sake. The framework does
  not judge which goals are valid.

- **The actual world.** The framework is built in and for a world structured by intersecting
  oppressive systems. These systems do not sit outside the framework as contextual notes. They
  are integral to explaining why certain things are difficult, inaccessible, or dangerous for
  specific people in specific contexts. The 16 systems identified as relevant appear throughout
  the framework where they act, rather than being collected into a separate section.

---

### Requirements

Not every association between minds constitutes a bond as this framework defines it. Three
minimum conditions must be met by each participant:

**1. Capacity for memory, prediction, and imagining the perspective of another.**

A mind must be able to hold representations of past interactions and use them to predict future
ones. It must also be able to model the interior experience of another — including states it has
not personally experienced, through imagination. Without memory, shared history cannot
accumulate. Without prediction, coupling cannot form. Without perspective-modeling, the
intimacy exchange breaks down: responsiveness requires some accurate inference about what
the other person is actually experiencing.

This requirement says nothing about *how* these capacities operate. They may work through
language, embodied pattern recognition, or any available channel. Minds differ significantly in
how they access and express these capacities — this is addressed in the section below.

**2. Capability and willingness to communicate through at least one shared channel.**

A channel is any medium through which one mind can transmit states to another: language,
gesture, touch, synchronized movement, written exchange, shared sensory experience. The
channel must be available to both parties, legible to both, and used without coercion.

Structural conditions — poverty, incarceration, geography, disability — can eliminate channels
that would otherwise be available. Oppressive systems determine which channels exist, at what
cost, and for whom.

**3. Capability and willingness to cooperate toward a shared goal.**

A bond requires some common orientation, even if that orientation is simply being together.
Purely adversarial or purely coerced relations do not qualify. A superior extracting labor from a
subordinate shares space and accumulates history with them, but their relation is not a bond in
this framework's sense: the subordinate's cooperation is extracted rather than chosen, and the
goal serves one party at the expense of the other.

### Explanations and Definitions of Neurotype and Sanity

This section requires care, because the framework's treatment of these concepts differs from
their common usage.

**The standard mind is a fiction.**

"Neurotypical" is supposed to name the standard case — the mind against which divergent
minds are measured. But no such standard was established by measuring actual minds and
finding a natural category. Research shows that autistic traits, ADHD traits, and related profiles
are distributed *normally across the entire human population*: there is no bimodal distribution,
no natural break separating a "typical" population from a "divergent" one. The boundary was
drawn by clinical and social consensus, not by the structure of human variation itself (Springer,
2019).

This is not a minor technical point. It means that "neurotypical" is a political category, not a
biological one — a line drawn to distinguish minds that function within socially enforced norms
from minds that do not. What is treated as standard is what is enforced as standard through two
of the 16 oppressive systems: **somatic normativity**, which demands that minds function in
socially approved ways, and **sanism**, its component that specifically polices emotional and
cognitive states.

**Trauma produces the same observable deviations as developmental conditions.**

Neurotype is detected through behavior and speech. The detection mechanism — what
clinicians and researchers actually measure — is how a mind communicates, processes social
information, regulates emotion, and tolerates sensory load. Complex trauma (C-PTSD) produces
hypervigilance, attention difficulties, sensory sensitivity, emotional dysregulation, and impaired
executive function. These presentations are functionally indistinguishable from profiles
associated with autism and ADHD at the level of behavioral observation — so much so that
clinical differential diagnosis between them is a recognized challenge, and misattribution
between trauma responses and developmental profiles is common (Neurolaunch, 2025;
Zacharias, 2025).

If trauma during developmental windows reshapes the brain in ways that are functionally
identical to developmental differences — and there is substantial evidence that it does — then
the distinction between "innate neurodivergence" and "trauma-acquired deviation" is not
meaningful at the level of how a mind actually operates in the world and in relationships.

The mechanism of both is the same: sustained experience under adverse conditions — whether
those conditions arise from how the brain is constituted or from what the world has done to it —
produces characteristic patterns of processing, expression, and relational behavior. The etiology
(origin) may differ. The functional reality, which is what a relationships framework must address,
is the same kind of thing.

**The framework's position.**

This framework does not ask what neurotype or diagnostic category a person belongs to. It
asks what channels are accessible to a given mind, at what cost, in a given context — and what
structural and developmental conditions have shaped that cost profile. The "standard mind"
disappears as a reference point. There is no standard-version section and no accommodation
section. Every element of the framework is written for the full space of minds that meet the
requirements.

The same logic applies to sanity. States that deviate from socially approved emotional and
cognitive functioning — psychosis, mania, severe dissociation — are pathologized by sanism, not
because they make someone less of a person, but because they fall outside what the dominant
social order can manage. This framework does not use sanity as a prerequisite. The requirements
are what they are: memory, prediction, perspective-modeling, a shared channel, willingness to
cooperate. A mind in a manic state may meet these requirements in specific windows; a mind in
a dissociative state may not at that moment. The requirements are the test, not the diagnostic
label.

## Foundations

Three foundations support any genuine bond. None of them alone is sufficient. Together, all three
produce what this framework calls a **living bond** — a connection that is active and ongoing,
not merely historical.

- **Predictive Coupling** is what makes being with a specific person feel different from being
  with anyone else — a baseline of mutual legibility and ease that comes from each mind having
  built an accurate working model of the other.

- **Perceived Partner Responsiveness** is what makes the bond feel safe and intimate — the
  felt sense, built through repeated exchange, that this person understands you, values you, and
  cares for your wellbeing.

- **Bidirectional Relational Schema** is what makes the bond durable — the accumulated shared
  history that has encoded the other person into each mind's model of itself and the world,
  in both directions.

### Predictive Coupling (Predictability)

#### Theoretical basis and the problem with "neural synchrony"

Research on neural synchrony has documented something real: when two people are engaged
together in shared activity, their nervous systems show correlated patterns of activation. They
are tracking each other. But "neural synchrony" is an inadequate description of what is actually
happening, for the reason the framework's originator identified: the meaning of neural activity
differs across minds, architectures, and processing styles.

The same activation pattern does not mean the same thing in a mind organized by
autism-typical sensory processing as in one organized through dominant social-mirroring
channels. A sentient machine and a human might show correlated activation charts while
working on a problem together, and this would tell us almost nothing about whether genuine
mutual modeling was occurring. Neural activity is the substrate, not the phenomenon. The
phenomenon worth preserving is **mutual predictability**.

Two minds in genuine coupling are each building and updating an accurate model of the other.
Each can anticipate the other's next state, response, or action with reasonable reliability. Being
together reduces mutual uncertainty: being with this person means I know approximately what
will happen next, and they know approximately what I will do. This does not require identical
neural patterns. It requires that the models each mind builds of the other are accurate enough
to make interaction feel less effortful than interaction with a stranger.

This reframing removes "neural" as the operative criterion and replaces it with the functional
criterion: are these minds becoming mutually legible to each other?

#### What coupling requires

Predictive coupling has three structural requirements, any one of which, if blocked, prevents
coupling from forming.

**Mutual attention.** Both minds must be attending to each other, not merely occupying the
same space. Obligatory co-presence — the same office, the same classroom — does not produce
coupling, because attention is directed not by choice but by constraint. Hall's (2019) research
confirms this empirically: hours spent in chosen, voluntary engagement predict relational
closeness; hours spent in obligatory environments predict it far less.

**Shared activation.** Both minds must be in similar enough affective or arousal states to
register each other's signals. A mind in a state of profound dissociation cannot couple at that
moment — not because it lacks the capacity, but because the channel through which the other
person would be registered is not currently accessible. A mind under active threat is attending
to the threat, not to the person. Shared activation does not mean identical states; it means
states close enough on the relevant register that exchange can occur.

**Enclosure.** A felt sense that this engagement is bounded — that it is *ours* rather than
parallel processing in the same environment. Without enclosure, coupling does not generate the
sense of a bond even if mutual modeling is occurring. Enclosure is produced by the voluntary
nature of the engagement, by shared focus, and by context that marks the interaction as
distinct from ordinary public life.

#### Why hierarchy prevents coupling

Hierarchy is structurally incompatible with predictive coupling. This is a core theoretical claim
of the framework.

Genuine coupling is symmetric: both minds build models of the other, and both models achieve
comparable accuracy. Under hierarchy, the subordinate observes the superior and may develop
an accurate predictive model — survival often requires this. But the superior's behavior is
governed by license: they can deviate from expectations without consequence, change the
terms of engagement unilaterally, and act in ways the subordinate has no power to anticipate
reliably. Meanwhile, the superior does not need to model the subordinate accurately; compliance
is extracted rather than earned through understanding.

The result is asymmetric modeling: the subordinate may predict the superior with some accuracy
because they must. The superior predicts the subordinate coarsely, if at all. This is not coupling.
It is a power relation in which modeling flows predominantly in one direction.

Each of the 16 oppressive systems generates hierarchical asymmetries that break coupling in
specific ways:

- **Rankism** structures deference so that the superior's behavior is unpredictable from
  below while the subordinate's compliance is predictable from above.
- **Adultism** makes the adult's decisions govern the child's conditions, making the adult's
  predictions of the child easier than the reverse.
- **Productivism** extracts labor time, leaving insufficient free time for the chosen, voluntary
  engagement through which coupling forms.
- **Enclosure** removes shared physical spaces and shared resources, forcing paid gatekeeping
  onto what were once accessible commons where people could meet as equals.
- **Territorial barriers** (produced by territorialism, immigration enforcement, incarceration)
  prevent co-presence entirely for those affected.
- **Cisheteropatriarchy** constrains what emotional and communicative expressions are legible
  across the gender divide, making suppressed parties harder to model accurately because they
  are required to suppress the states that would make them legible.
- **Somatic normativity** makes minds that process information differently appear erratic or
  unreadable to those who process along socially enforced channels — not because they are
  unpredictable, but because the observer lacks the model to read them.
- **Colonial residue**, including linguicism, renders minds whose primary languages and
  epistemic frameworks differ from the dominant ones as less legible — harder to model — not
  because their behavior is incoherent, but because the observer's models are colonial-calibrated.

In every case, the oppressive system introduces an asymmetry that prevents the mutual
modeling that coupling requires. This is why the framework requires cooperation toward a
shared goal and rejects coerced or purely hierarchical relations.

#### Coupling in groups

Predictive coupling in groups larger than two operates at multiple levels simultaneously. Each
pair within the group builds its own dyadic coupling through shared attention and mutual
modeling. And the group as a whole builds coupling that cannot be reduced to any individual
dyad — a collective legibility in which each member can anticipate, with reasonable accuracy,
the group's next state, not just each individual's.

Research on multi-person physiological synchrony confirms that group-level cohesion emerges
from whole-group synchrony, and is distinct from the sum of dyadic bonds (Vicaria & Dickens,
2016; Frontiers in Physiology, 2020). Strong dyadic pairs within a group do not guarantee
whole-group coupling. Weak individual pairs can coexist with strong group-level coupling, as in
collectives unified by shared purpose rather than by one-on-one familiarity.

The framework treats both levels as real and distinct. A bond between four people includes the
four dyadic couplings and the one whole-group coupling — five distinct things, each requiring
its own shared attention, shared activation, and enclosure.

### Perceived Partner Responsiveness (Intimacy)

#### Theoretical basis and the need for refinement

Perceived partner responsiveness (PPR) was developed by Reis and Shaver (1988) as the
central mechanism through which intimacy develops. The formulation: Person A discloses
something — makes themselves visible — Person B responds, and Person A perceives that
response as evidence that Person B sees them accurately, values them, and cares for their
wellbeing. When this perception recurs across interactions, intimacy deepens.

Two refinements are necessary.

**First:** the terms used to describe the exchange — "expression," "understanding,"
"validation," "care" — are not universal. What constitutes expression, what reads as validation,
what conveys care, all depend on how a given mind is organized and what channels structural
conditions have left available. Direct eye contact signals responsiveness in one context and
aggression in another. Silence signals contemplation or dismissal depending on the history.
Physical touch signals safety or threat depending on what the body has been taught. Indirect
communication — saying "I'm tired" when the actual need is "I want to leave" or "hold me" —
may look like avoidance, but it is often a probe: testing whether the environment is safe enough
for the more direct version.

Several oppressive systems produce specific patterns of illegibility that the framework must
account for. **Colonial residue** makes some forms of expression read as deficient or
inarticulate when they are not. **Sanism** makes emotional states outside approved ranges hard
for others to receive as valid. **Cisheteropatriarchy** shapes which expressions of need are
legible as legitimate and which are dismissed as weakness or excess. **Rankism** determines
whose disclosures are worth responding to and whose are not.

**Second:** PPR is a perception, not a behavior. What matters is what Person A perceives, not
what Person B objectively did. This matters in two directions. It means that PPR can be built or
undermined through communication — the same act can register as responsive or dismissive
depending on context, history, and what the perceiver brings. And it means that PPR is
vulnerable to distortion: a history of threat exposure can cause a mind to perceive care signals
as hostile, even when they are genuine, because it has learned that such signals were
historically followed by harm. This is not irrationality; it is accurate learning from a dangerous
past. The framework does not pathologize this. It recognizes it as a structural effect of sustained
oppressive conditions.

#### The awareness prior to all exchanges

For any exchange to succeed, each mind must have sufficient contact with three things:

- Its own current state: what it is feeling, what it needs, what it is capable of in this moment.
- A model of the other's current state: what they are expressing, what they might need, what
  they can give right now.
- A reading of what this particular context allows: not every environment is safe for every
  kind of expression.

This awareness is prior to and necessary for every exchange. It is also exactly where the full
range of mind variation matters. Minds that have difficulty accessing internal states — whether
due to alexithymia (difficulty identifying emotions), dissociation, trauma numbing, or other
causes — have less to work with when initiating exchanges. Minds that have difficulty reading
others through the dominant channels (facial expression, vocal tone) require more explicit
information to work from. This is not a deficit to be corrected; it is a cost profile to be
accommodated by the structure of the exchange itself.

The five awareness modes — self-awareness, awareness of the other, contextual awareness,
structural awareness (which oppressive systems are currently active in this context), and
processing awareness (what each mind can currently handle) — are not background conditions.
They are active operations that both parties must sustain throughout any genuine exchange.

#### The exchanges that build PPR

PPR is built through a repeating set of exchanges. Each exchange is a distinct call-and-response
unit with its own function, its own typical failure modes, and its own dependence on the
awareness operations described above. The exchanges can occur separately or together; they
do not require a fixed sequence, though they tend to deepen in the order given when a bond is
forming.

**Exchange 1: Expression → Safety**

Person A signals their current emotional state — not necessarily in words, not necessarily
directly. The signal may be facial, vocal, physical, written, or behavioral. Its form depends on
which channels are accessible and affordable for this mind at this moment. Indirect expression
— showing withdrawal, showing irritability, showing a change in energy — is still expression.
It may be all the expression available in a context where more direct forms carry too much risk.

Person B's response functions to reduce Person A's perception of threat — affirming, by any
legible means, that this expression was received without punishment and that the relationship
is safe enough for this state to have been shown. The functional goal is reduction of threat
perception. The form it takes is not fixed: for some minds, synchronized expression and eye
contact achieves this; for others, verbal reassurance; for others, stillness and non-withdrawal;
for others, responsive touch; for others, written acknowledgment. What matters is the
function, not the channel.

Failure in this exchange: Person B responds in a way that confirms the risk — critiques the
expression, withdraws, responds with discomfort, or simply ignores it. The effect is that future
expressions become more guarded, more indirect, or absent.

**Exchange 2: Disclosure → Validation**

Person A describes their internal state and its context in words: not only "I feel afraid" but
"I feel afraid because this situation recalls a time when..." The verbal specificity matters.
It gives Person B something concrete to respond to. Without it, Person B can only respond
to a general emotional signal, which limits how accurate the response can be.

Person B's response validates: it conveys that the disclosure makes sense, that the emotional
experience is real, that the self-understanding is accurate. This is distinct from offering
solutions, from reframing ("have you considered that..."), from correcting, from offering a
similar experience in return as the primary response. Validation says: *this is understandable;
this is real.* It does not move the conversation forward until this landing has been made.

A structural problem that multiple oppressive systems create here is that they produce
experiences for which the dominant framework offers no validation. A person describing what
sustained oppression does to their interior life may find their listener responding from a
framework that does not contain that experience as a legitimate category. "You're reading too
much into it" is not validation; it is the erasure of an accurate account.

Failure in this exchange: Person B offers solutions, comparisons, corrections, or reframings
before or instead of validation. Person A experiences not being understood and contracts their
future disclosures accordingly.

**Exchange 3: Asking → Giving**

Person A names a specific need or want: a request for help, a limit, a preference, a "no."
Person B responds with what was actually asked for, in a way that supports Person A's agency.

Several distinctions matter here. Emotional support (validation, being heard) and instrumental
support (practical help, advice, problem-solving) are different types of response to different
types of need. Giving the wrong type — advice when what was needed was validation, or
validation when what was needed was practical action — fails the exchange regardless of how
generous the intent. Giving too much, too visibly, or in a way that makes the asker feel
incompetent also fails it.

Asking is itself difficult. Multiple oppressive systems constrain who is permitted to ask, what
kinds of needs are treated as legitimate, and what happens to people who ask directly.
**Rankism** treats requests from lower-status people as impositions. **Cisheteropatriarchy**
treats emotional needs in men as signs of weakness and boundary-setting in women as
aggression. **Adultism** treats young people's needs as negotiable at adult discretion. The
effect is that for many people, asking directly is a learned risk, and the indirect
communication that replaces it — which looks like hinting or vagueness — is rational
self-protection, not a communication skill deficit.

Over many exchanges across many interactions, the pattern of asking and giving should
roughly balance — not through tallying, which corrodes trust by turning generosity into
accounting, but naturally. Sustained one-directional giving, where the same person is always
giving and the same person always taking, damages both the giver and the taker, and
damages the bond.

**Expression of closeness**

After an exchange — particularly after Person B has responded well — Person A acknowledges
having received it: through gratitude, through reciprocal vulnerability, through any expression
that closes the circuit. This is not a separate exchange so much as the closing of the one just
completed. Its absence leaves the exchange unfinished and deprives Person B of the signal
that the response registered and was valued. Over time, consistently unclosed exchanges
reduce Person B's motivation to respond, for the same reason repeated unanswered efforts
reduce anyone's willingness to continue.

#### The Matthew effect inside PPR

When exchange attempts fail consistently — when expressions are not met with safety, when
disclosures are not validated, when asks go unresponded to — a mind learns to stop making
the attempt. Not through decision, but through the same mechanism of adaptive disengagement
documented in the neurodivergent learning literature (Jones, Rauwolf, & Westermann, 2025):
when the expected cost of attempting exceeds the expected return, investment withdraws.

The result is a progressive narrowing. Fewer expressions → less for Person B to respond to →
fewer chances to respond well → further narrowing of expression. A bond that looks stable
from the outside may have contracted significantly at the intimacy level without either party
having made any explicit decision to withdraw.

#### PPR in groups

In a group, PPR operates at the dyadic level between each pair and at the group level between
each member and the group as a whole. Group-level PPR is whether each member feels that the
group as an entity sees them accurately, values them, and cares for their wellbeing — not only
whether specific individuals within the group do. These are distinct. A member may feel deeply
known by one other member and invisible to the group as a collective. Group-level PPR requires
the group to create conditions for expression, to respond when members make themselves
visible, and to distribute giving across members without consistent asymmetry.

### Bidirectional Relational Schema (Familiarity)

#### Theoretical basis

Baldwin (1992) introduced the relational schema as a cognitive structure representing
regularities in patterns of interpersonal relatedness — an organized mental model of how a
specific relationship works. It contains three elements: a self-schema (who I am in this
relationship), an other-schema (who they are to me), and an interpersonal script (what patterns
of exchange we fall into together). The schema is built through repeated, significant experience
with a specific other, and it shapes how new information about that other is interpreted,
remembered, and acted upon.

The concept is sound. The problem is abstraction: "cognitive structure representing
regularities in patterns of interpersonal relatedness" is accurate but not graspable. The
word "familiarity" — in the framework title — grounds it. A relational schema is the
accumulated familiarity that makes a person feel known, makes interactions with them feel
intelligible without effort, and makes them difficult to disentangle from your understanding of
yourself. This is what pop culture recognizes when it calls something a "relationship" — not
any bond, but one in which this level of familiarity has been established.

What builds schema is not time alone. It is distinctive shared history: experiences that were
emotionally significant, novel, and self-expanding for the people involved. Aron et al.'s
self-expansion theory (1992, 2000) establishes that people encode others into their self-concept
when those people are present during experiences that expanded the self — when who I am
changed in the presence of this person. Routine co-presence does not build schema at the same
rate as experiences that are genuinely new, genuinely challenging, and emotionally significant.

#### Bidirectionality: the critical addition

The existing theory does not require that a relational schema be built mutually. Someone can
develop a detailed, emotionally intense relational schema for another person who has barely
registered their existence. This is precisely the failure mode the framework is designed to
prevent.

A relational schema is only a bond's foundation, in this framework's terms, if it has been built
in both directions. Both minds must have accumulated comparable distinctive shared history.
Both must have encoded the other into their self-concept. Both must have developed compatible
sets of relational expectations — compatible scripts for how this relationship works.

If only one mind has undergone this process, what exists is not a shared foundation but a
private projection. One person has a relationship. The other has an acquaintance.

#### Limerence: the failure mode of unidirectional schema

Limerence (Tennov, 1979) is the condition that arises when relational schema builds
asymmetrically. The limerent person develops an intense, obsessive preoccupation with
another — a deep, detailed, emotionally governing model of that person — accompanied by an
acute longing for reciprocation, idealization, and emotional dependence whose intensity
fluctuates with perceived signals of reciprocation or rejection.

The mechanism: one person undergoes significant self-expansion through experiences involving
another. The other person becomes encoded as the agent of that growth — part of how the first
person understands who they are becoming. But if the experiences were not equally expanding
for the second person — if only one mind changed significantly through those encounters — the
schema builds asymmetrically. One mind has incorporated the other into its self-concept; the
other has not.

Uncertainty amplifies this. The core experience of limerence is not love, which is compatible
with knowing and being known, but the desire to be desired — a state that feeds specifically on
ambiguity about reciprocation. Sufficient ambiguity keeps the obsessive cycle running. Clear
rejection ends it; clear reciprocation transforms it into something that may or may not be love.
Limerence is sustained by the unresolved in-between, which means situations that provide
intermittent signals of possible reciprocation are its most reliable fuel.

This is not a character flaw of the limerent person. It is what happens when self-expansion
occurs asymmetrically and the structural conditions — social proximity, shared environments,
ambiguous signals — keep the possibility of reciprocation plausibly alive.

#### Bidirectionality as structural prevention

The bidirectionality requirement prevents limerence by intervening at the point of construction,
not after the obsessive state has already formed.

Before investing in shared history-building, the framework requires verification that experiences
are genuinely novel and engaging for both minds — not just for one. This requires explicit
checking: before a joint activity, has each person actually not done this before? During: is each
person being challenged, or is one performing familiarity for the other's development? After:
can each person report what they discovered or how they were changed?

If only one mind expanded, the activity did not produce shared schema material. It produced
self-expansion for one person with another person present. These are different, and the
difference is the structural cause of limerence.

#### How oppressive systems create asymmetric schema

Several oppressive systems systematically produce asymmetric experiences of the same events.

**Rankism** means that the employer and employee at the same company gathering do not have
the same experience of it. The employer is at ease; the employee is managing the interaction.
One is observed; the other observes. The schema they each build from that encounter, even if
they were in the same room for the same duration, is different in content and significance.

**Adultism** means that the adult and the child in the same learning situation are having
fundamentally different experiences. The adult may find novelty; the child may be performing
deference. The adult encodes growth; the child encodes compliance. This is not mutual
self-expansion.

**Cisheteropatriarchy** means that in many mixed-gender interactions, one party is managing
social threat and the other is not. The experience is structurally different, and the schema that
results from it differs accordingly.

The bidirectionality requirement exposes these asymmetries. If, in a pattern of interactions,
experiences are consistently more novel, more challenging, and more growth-producing for one
party than the other, the relationship is structured by hierarchy — and the framework's
requirement is not met.

#### Schema in groups

In a group, relational schemas operate at two distinct levels.

Each member holds a dyadic schema for each other member — a separate, distinct model of
"who I am with this specific person" and "what patterns of exchange we fall into." These dyadic
schemas can differ significantly from one another even within the same person: the same
individual may feel expansive and known in their dyadic bond with one group member and
constricted and unseen in their dyadic bond with another.

The group also builds a collective schema — an organized model of "who we are together" —
which is not reducible to the sum of any pair's dyadic schemas. The collective schema is encoded
through experiences that involve all members simultaneously: moments of shared difficulty,
shared discovery, shared joy, shared loss. These are the events from which "remember when
all of us..." is told, and that telling is itself schema maintenance.

Bidirectionality at the group level requires that group-level experiences are equally expanding
and encoding for all members — that no member is consistently the audience for others'
growth, the witness to others' moments, the one who facilitates while others experience.
