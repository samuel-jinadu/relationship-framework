# A Relationships Framework

---

## What This Is

A framework for building and understanding personally-bonded groups of any size — two
people, three people, a polycule, a close friend group, a chosen family. It applies to any
configuration of people who matter to each other. Romantic relationships are one instance,
not the center, not the most important case, not the model everything else is compared to.

What most people call "a relationship" is really just *shared history* — the sense that we
have a past together, mutual expectations, a "we." That is real and it matters. But a bond
can have shared history while still being essentially empty: the structure is there, the
familiarity is there, but something that makes it *live* is missing. This framework names
what that something is and how to build it.

---

## The Actual Context

This framework operates under conditions as they actually exist, not as they ideally would.
Sixteen interlocking systems shape what is possible, what is safe, what costs what, and who
carries what load in any relationship:

1. **Rankism** — your position in a hierarchy determines your worth; lower-ranked people's
   deference, labour, and ideas are extracted upward
2. **Adultism** — youth are treated as problems to manage rather than people to consult;
   their time, compliance, and futures are extracted by adults
3. **Kinship normativity** — only blood, marriage, and reproductive family structures are
   recognised; women's bodies and the financial resources of successful members are extracted
4. **Cisheteropatriarchy** — three interlocking systems enforcing the gender binary, compulsory
   heterosexuality and reproduction, and male authority; extracts domestic labour, safety,
   autonomy, and sexual access
5. **Ethnic majoritarianism** — ethnic hierarchy determines what people deserve; natural
   resources, political representation, and economic opportunity are extracted from minority groups
6. **Territorialism** — a national border determines your worth; freedom of movement, migrant
   labour, and land rights are extracted
7. **Religious dominance** — alignment with the locally powerful religion determines access
   to safety and public life; financial resources and epistemic freedom are extracted
8. **Enclosure** — shared commons are converted to private property; what previously belonged
   to everyone becomes inaccessible to most
9. **Productivism** — a person's worth equals what they measurably produce; maximum output
   is extracted from bodies and time, and those who cannot produce are invalidated
10. **Extractivism** — large-scale resource removal for external markets; local health,
    livelihoods, and futures are extracted and the damage is made irreversible
11. **Colonial residue** — colonial hierarchy persisting in bodies, languages, and knowledge;
    includes epistemic oppression (Western knowledge as default), linguicism (English as
    intelligence), colorism, and texturism
12. **Somatic normativity** — bodies and minds must look and function in approved ways or
    be treated as less than human; includes ableism, sanism (mental/emotional normalcy),
    and lookism
13. **Punitivism** — the correct response to harm is more harm; includes both state
    punishment and mob violence; liberty, physical integrity, and life are extracted from
    the accused
14. **Amatonormativity** — the only fully legitimate life is organised around an exclusive
    romantic long-term relationship; social recognition is extracted from everyone who does
    not conform to this structure
15. **Compulsory existence** — the obligation to be alive is enforced through criminalisation,
    religious condemnation, and forced hospitalisation; agency over one's own existence is
    extracted
16. **Meritism** — capturing collective attention grants social licence and exemption from
    ordinary rules; equal application of law and accountability is extracted from those
    without public significance

These systems do not add up separately. They interact. A person under several of them
simultaneously is not experiencing their sum — they are experiencing something qualitatively
different that has its own texture, its own cost structure, and its own specific effect on
what relationships can and cannot do.

These systems are the actual context. They are not background noise that can be set aside
when discussing relationships. They determine the real cost of openness, the real safety of
disclosure, the real availability of shared activities, and the real trustworthiness of safety
signals. Every difficulty this framework describes has a structural source in one or more of
these systems.

## The Three Foundations

A live bond requires three things. They operate independently — you can have one without
the others, which is why relational problems feel specific rather than just "not working."

---

### Foundation 1: Predictive Coupling

Two or more nervous systems becoming mutually legible to each other through shared
activity — each learning to anticipate the others, each becoming predictable enough that
being together produces ease rather than effort. The most functional form is not sameness
but complementary fit: different people playing roles that work together.

This does not require any specific channel. Eye contact, matched facial expressions, and
synchronized speech are *one* set of signals through which this can develop — but they
are not the only signals, and not every nervous system processes them at the same cost.
Parallel engagement in a shared domain, joint physical activity, complementary action
during a task, shared sensory environments — all of these produce genuine coupling.
The test for any activity is functional: does it produce mutual attention, shared activation,
and some sense of enclosure — a felt sense that this is happening *between us*? If yes,
coupling can develop regardless of the form it takes.

In any group, coupling operates at three levels simultaneously: between each pair of
people within the group, across the whole group as a unit, and within each individual's
general capacity to attune. These levels are independent. Deep dyadic coupling between
two people does not automatically produce group-level coupling. That requires activities
involving the whole group.

**Coupling ends when the shared activity ends.** It does not persist. It is rebuilt each time.
What accumulates is the ease with which it re-establishes — nervous systems that have
coupled many times need less activation to re-entrain.

**When coupling breaks:** One or both nervous systems now associate shared presence with
threat. Conversation will not repair this before the body does. First, individual regulation —
separate space, time. Then re-entry through low-stakes shared activity with no emotional
expectation. Then gradually increasing the weight of the activity as ease re-establishes.
Not the other way around.

---

### Foundation 2: Perceived Partner Responsiveness (PPR)

The felt sense, built through repeated interaction, that the other person(s) *accurately*
perceive your internal state, treat it as genuinely mattering, and act in your interest. The
key word is accurately. Warmth without accuracy is not PPR — it is a warm response to
what someone assumed you meant. PPR requires that what you actually expressed is what
got responded to.

PPR is not a permanent state. It is produced through cycles of expression and response
and maintained through those cycles continuing. A long history of good cycles creates a
deep sense of being known — but this can be sustained by the *expectation* of good
responses as easily as by actual attunement, which means it can be wrong. The test is
accuracy, not comfort.

**When PPR breaks:** The specific mismatch must be named — not just "I'm sorry" but
"I heard you needed to be understood and I tried to fix it instead" or "you were telling me
something real and I made it smaller." Without naming the mismatch, the accuracy problem
has not been addressed and nothing has been repaired.

---

### Foundation 3: Relational Schema

An active working model of how the people in this bond function together. Not just stored
memory but a predictive structure: if-then scripts ("when I show X, they respond with Y"),
a sense of identity inside the bond ("this is who I am with them"), and episodic memories
organized around peak moments and shared narrative. Schema is what makes it possible to
interpret new interactions without starting from zero every time.

**Bidirectionality is built into the definition.** A schema built by one person about another
who is not building one in return is not a relational schema. It is a private projection. The
person who is building alone is constructing an imagined relationship, not participating in
a real one. This is the structural condition for limerence — intense one-sided attachment
to someone who is largely unaware of the depth of feeling directed at them. Bidirectionality
is checked actively, not assumed from the other person's presence.

In any group, schema operates at two levels: a separate schema exists between each pair of
people, and a group schema exists for the collective "us." These are distinct and built
differently. Stories between two people build their dyadic schema. "Remember when all of
us..." builds group schema. Both are needed; they cannot substitute for each other.

**When part of a group dissolves:** The group schema disappears. What was partly
maintained by group-level belonging gets exposed, and the remaining bonds discover what
they are when standing alone. A pair who looked close because they were part of something
together may find that the dyadic foundations between them were thinner than the group
made them appear.

**Undo / ending:** Stop shared reminiscing — this removes maintenance and allows the
schema to begin losing coherence. Reclaim memories as self-memories: "I grew during
that period; they were present, but the growth was mine." Create contradictory evidence by
expressing something new and observing whether the response fits who you actually are.
Begin building schema with others, which produces new self-referential encoding that
displaces the old shared overlap.

## Building Each Foundation

---

### Building Predictive Coupling

Activities that generate coupling share a structure: mutual attention, shared activation,
and some enclosure — a felt sense that this is *ours*. The specific form depends on which
channels are affordable for the nervous systems involved and what structural access allows.

**Activation — produces shared arousal and mutual focus:**
- Games: any activity requiring real-time reading and prediction of the other's moves,
  competitive or cooperative
- Making things together: cooking, building, music, any joint creation where each
  person's contribution is legible and matters to the outcome
- Moving together: dancing, walking in rhythm, any synchronized or complementary
  physical activity
- High-activation content: watching things together that produce shared spikes of
  emotion — thriller, horror, comedy, anything overwhelming or funny
- Drinking together
- Erotic and sexual activities (for bonds where this is present and consented to)

**Atmosphere — creates a shared sensory context:**
- Eating together with deliberate setting rather than convenience
- Shared sensory environments: fire, water, particular light or sound conditions
- Shared aesthetic experience: concert, storm, view — anything beautiful or overwhelming
  that happens to both of you simultaneously
- Close physical co-presence without task: resting in the same space, intimate physical
  care — touch, holding, grooming
- Dancing closely, physically coordinated movement without external goal

**Prospective — builds anticipation, which generates mild activation before the activity:**
- Planning a shared activity together
- Imagining shared future scenarios out loud
- Talking about doing something together before doing it

**On time:** Chosen leisure activities with genuine mutual engagement: roughly 40–60 hours
for a casual bond to form, 80–100 for a clear friendship, 120–200 or more for a close one.
These thresholds apply to *chosen* activity. Obligatory co-presence — same workplace,
same class, thrown together by circumstance — accumulates hours at a much lower rate
because the attention is not mutually invested.

**When channels are blocked:** Structural isolation — produced by poverty, disability,
geography, immigration status, incarceration, and other systemic conditions — blocks
physical access to shared space. Online co-operative games, shared streaming with live
commentary, working on something together over a call — these are genuine coupling
activities with some channels unavailable, not lesser substitutes. Which channels matter
most varies per nervous system. What structural oppression does is remove options, forcing
people into forms they may or may not be well-suited to.

---

### Building Perceived Partner Responsiveness

Every disclosure is a risk calculation, not a free choice. Whether to express something,
how much, how fast, and through which channel depends on an honest assessment of what
it will cost if it goes wrong. The response to that expression is likewise not a one-sided
listening skill — it is bidirectional attunement: both people learning each other's encoding
systems, which may be genuinely different.

---

**Step 0: Establish safety explicitly — before anything else**

Standard social signals of safety — eye contact, mirroring, calm tone, physical proximity
— do not communicate safety to every nervous system. For nervous systems calibrated by
sustained exposure to threat, these signals may be exactly what was present before previous
violations. For nervous systems that process non-verbal information at high cost, these
signals require translation that depletes the capacity needed for the actual conversation.

Safety must be negotiated explicitly, not assumed. This is not a romantic moment — it is
the prerequisite the original intimacy research always assumed but never named.

Work out, in advance of any disclosure cycle:

*What safety looks and feels like for each person:*
Does eye contact feel connecting or intrusive? Do you need verbal check-ins or quiet
presence? Is touch welcome, and which forms? Do you need time before responding, or does
silence feel like abandonment? How do you know when the other person is actually present
and tracking you, if not through the standard signals?

*What signals danger:*
What topics or communication styles trigger a high threat response? What past relational
injuries does the other person need to know about? What happens when you hit overwhelm
and need to stop — what does that look like, and what do you need when it happens?

*What the environment needs:*
Noise level, sensory load, privacy, time structure, whether external stressors are currently
too high for this conversation to be productive at all.

---

**Cycle 1: Threat-calibrated expression → Safety provision**

The expressing person (A) communicates internal state through a channel that is low-cost
for them. This might be:

- *Explicit verbal:* "I'm scared and I need reassurance" — the most legible form, and the
  one with the highest cost under threat
- *Implicit verbal:* "I'm tired," "I'm not sure about that" — these are not communication
  failures; they are probes into safety, testing whether the other person will notice without
  requiring full exposure
- *Written:* Same content, more time to find the words, no real-time performance pressure
- *Action-based:* Showing up, doing something, making the effort visible rather than stating
  it
- *Parallel activity:* What comes out in conversation while doing something else together,
  where the reduced face-to-face pressure makes expression possible that wouldn't happen
  otherwise

When expression comes through an unexpected channel, the first question is: **am I reading
the right channel?** Not: why didn't they just say it clearly?

When expression is indirect — "I'm tired," "maybe later," "I don't know" — this is data
about the current state of safety, not a communication problem to fix. The person is
probing: *will you notice my state without me having to expose it directly?* The response
is to treat the indirect as if it were direct: "You sound tired — do you need to stop?" This
demonstrates that directness is safe without demanding it. Over time, as safety accumulates,
indirect expression may shift toward directness on its own.

Person B's job in this cycle is safety provision: respond in whatever way reduces the other
person's perception of threat and signals that the next, more exposed expression would be
safe to attempt. What this looks like is what was established in the explicit negotiation, not
what social convention assumes.

---

**Cycle 2: Bidirectional attunement → Validation**

Person A expresses what they are experiencing. The more context they can give, the more
Person B has to work with — "I feel scared because I might lose this job" is more workable
than "I feel scared." But some people genuinely cannot reliably identify what they are
feeling. This is not avoidance or suppression; it is a real gap in access to internal states.
Alternative forms of expression are valid: "I don't know what I'm feeling, but my body is
doing X," "I'm drawn away from this situation," "something is wrong but I can't name it."

Person B's task is to attune to what was *actually* expressed — not to what they expected,
not to what they assumed is happening. Two people may genuinely use different encoding
systems: the way one person signals distress may look unremarkable to someone calibrated
for different signals, and the way one person demonstrates care may be invisible to someone
looking for different forms. Learning each other's encoding is both people's work, not one
person's failure.

**Validation** means: conveying that what was expressed makes sense, that the experience
is real and legitimate. It does not mean agreeing with their conclusions. It does not mean
reassuring them that everything will be fine. It does not mean offering solutions.

The most common failure: providing instrumental support (analysis, advice, problem-solving)
when what was needed was emotional acknowledgment. These are different categories. The
wrong type of support, delivered consistently, communicates that the other person's
emotional reality is not what this relationship is for. Mismatching support type reliably
produces worse outcomes than doing nothing.

When the expressing person cannot identify their feeling: validate the confusion itself. "I
hear that you don't know what you're feeling. That's real. You don't have to know for me
to stay present."

When Person B sees that the other person has been translating their expression into legible
form — masking, performing expected responses, spending energy on making themselves
readable — naming that cost matters: "I notice you've been working hard just to show up
to this conversation. Thank you. You don't have to keep doing that here."

---

**Cycle 3: Effort-aware giving → Explicit ask and response**

This cycle handles specific needs: I need help with X, I don't want to do Y, I need more
time, I need you to hold this and not fix it. Asking may still be indirect at this stage — the
person is still testing whether the safety from earlier cycles extends to direct requests.

Effort-aware giving accounts for what the asking person has already spent before the ask
arrived. Managing environmental load, translating internal experience into expressible form,
assessing whether it is safe to ask, deciding whether the cost of asking is worth it — all of
this happens before the words come out. Effort-aware giving looks like:

- Reducing the demand on them to perform wellness: "You don't have to be recovering
  well for my sake"
- Offering before the ask, where visible: "I notice you're at capacity — I'm reducing the
  noise"
- Not requiring them to explain themselves fully to receive support
- Recognizing that they may have less capacity for gratitude or reciprocal support in this
  moment than usual — and that this is a function of what they are currently carrying, not
  a reflection of how they feel about the relationship

**Reciprocity over time is equity of effort, not equality of behavior.** Visible behavior is
not all the work being done. Managing translation (converting authentic internal expression
into forms the other person can receive), sustaining presence through high sensory load,
holding a conversation while managing other processing demands — all of this is effort,
none of it is visible, and all of it counts. True reciprocity is tracked over months and
seasons. Some seasons one person is in crisis and giving less. Some seasons the other is.
The balance is tracked over the arc of the relationship, not interaction by interaction. When
indirect asking returns after a period of directness, that is not regression — it is information
that something has shifted in the safety conditions: "I'm hearing indirectness again. Something
changed. Let's find out what."

---

**Cycle 4: Expression of closeness**

Person A registers what just happened — any expression that closes the circuit and signals
that the responsiveness was felt. This ranges from a simple "thank you" to explicit expressions
of love to whatever forms are natural in this bond. This also functions as a vulnerable
expression in its own right, as safety provision, and sometimes as an ask for reciprocal
acknowledgment. Its function is determined by context, not by the words.

---

**The Matthew effect in PPR:** When bids for closeness are missed — because they came
through an unexpected channel, because the response was wrong for the need, because the
cycle failed — the person becomes less likely to try again. The other person does not know
a bid was made and wonders why the first person is withdrawing. Without interruption,
both people progressively retreat until intimacy feels impossible. The intervention is to
make the structure visible early: "I think I missed something — was that an attempt at
closeness that I didn't respond to well?"

---

**Five awareness modes:**

1. *Self-awareness:* What is my actual internal state, right now? What do I need? What is
   my current processing capacity? (Including: I don't know what I'm feeling, and that is
   real information too — not a failure of self-knowledge but a present-tense fact about
   interoceptive access.)

2. *Other-awareness:* What is their actual state, read through the channel they actually
   use? What have they already spent before this moment? What are they carrying from
   outside this conversation?

3. *Processing awareness:* Live, moment-to-moment information. Right now my capacity
   for verbal processing is low. Right now I need movement to stay regulated. Right now
   my social battery is depleted and parallel time will rebuild it before focused connection
   is possible again. This is distinct from a diagnosis — it is present-tense information
   about current capacity, not a fixed category.

4. *Structural awareness:* Which of the 16 systems are actively running in this interaction?
   What is each person being extracted from right now? When something feels like it is
   about the two of you, is it possible it is actually a system running and both of you are
   under its pressure? Named systems stop being blamed on the relationship. "This isn't us
   failing — this is productivism and rankism in the room, and we're both exhausted by it."

5. *Contextual awareness:* What is the broader relational history shaping this moment?
   What is the external environment demanding from each person? What else is happening
   that this conversation is inside?

### Building Relational Schema

**Bidirectional self-expansion:**

Do things together that are genuinely new to everyone involved and require real effort from
all of them. Novelty during challenge produces strong memory encoding. When the other
person is the agent of that challenge — when your growth happened *because* they were
there, doing it too — they get encoded into your self-concept.

The bidirectionality check runs before, during, and after every schema-building activity:

- *Before:* Has everyone actually not done this? For real?
- *During:* Is everyone being challenged, or are some people performing expertise while
  others struggle?
- *After:* What did each person discover that they didn't know before?

One-sided novelty — one person an expert, one person learning — does not build relational
schema between them. It builds self-expansion for the learner and a teaching performance
for the expert. Over time, the learner associates the expert with growth and may develop
deep one-sided attachment. The expert may feel needed but not *known*. This is the
structural setup for limerence, not for mutual bond.

In groups: the check runs across all members. If two expand and one does not, the group
schema does not build for that third person even if their dyadic schemas with the others
deepen.

**Shared reminiscing:**

After experiences, return to them together: "remember when we first tried that and couldn't
figure it out," especially any exchange that produces shared laughter. This converts episodic
memory into shared autobiographical narrative — which is the actual content of the schema.
Without active reminiscing, memories recategorize over time from "ours" to "mine, at a time
when they were present."

Reminiscing is maintenance, not nostalgia. Group reminiscing builds group schema. Dyadic
reminiscing builds dyadic schema. They build different things and cannot substitute for
each other.

## What Makes Building Harder

Whether a given practice produces the foundation it is aimed at or costs too much to get
off the ground depends on two things: **modifiers** (factors in the specific exchange or
relationship) and **structural load** (the ongoing extraction cost of the systems listed above).
These are not separate — structural load is what produces the modifiers.

| Foundation | What helps | What hinders |
|---|---|---|
| **Coupling** | Mutual genuine interest; channel cost manageable for everyone; chosen activity; complementary roles | Obligatory co-presence; one-sided engagement; high channel cost for any participant; forced form |
| **PPR** | Safety established explicitly; structural load currently manageable; expression matched to receiving capacity; support type matched to need | Safety assumed not established; high load; expression channel not readable to receiver; wrong support type |
| **Schema** | Novelty genuine for everyone; mutual challenge; emotional peaks; active reminiscing; bidirectionality verified | One-sided novelty; expert-learner dynamic; flat routine; no reminiscing; bidirectionality unverified |

**Structural load as a cost multiplier:** Each of the 16 systems, when actively running
on a person, increases the cost of building each foundation and lowers the threshold for
the hindrance column. Rankism running means disclosure is more dangerous. Somatic
normativity running means the body being involved in coupling carries an additional threat
of judgment. Amatonormativity running means the bond being non-romantic incurs social
penalties. Productivism running means neither person has the time or energy the activities
require. These are not incidental — they are the actual conditions under which most people
attempt to build bonds.

**Nervous system variation:** Every nervous system has a cost profile for different channels
and activities. These profiles are shaped by both inherent developmental factors and
cumulative experience — including sustained exposure to the systems above — and these
two sources are not separable. What matters for building is not the origin but the practical
effect: which channels are high-cost right now, which activities are accessible, which forms
of expression and response will actually work. No default form is assumed.

## Repair

Repair is the restoration of a foundation that was real, mutual, and got damaged. It is not
the same as undo, which is the intentional deconstruction of a foundation that was never
mutual or is no longer wanted.

Healthy bonds are approximately 70% rupture-and-repair, 30% smooth attunement. Repair
is not a sign that something is wrong — it is how trust deepens. A history of successful
repairs is schema content: evidence that this bond survives disconnection. The rupture alone
does not cause damage. Rupture without any attempt at repair does.

**Repair conversations move through four patterns:**

Research on conflict conversations identifies four recurring patterns in how repair exchanges
actually unfold. These are not stages you design — they are patterns that emerge. Recognizing
them tells you where a conversation is and what it needs to move.

| Pattern | What it looks like | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Exposition** | One-sided, long turns; complaint, confrontation, or hurt expressed; the other person mostly receiving | Expresses the grievance — necessary, cannot be skipped; associated with elevated fear, sadness, and stress |
| **Elicitation** | Questions that draw out the full impact; the person who caused the rupture asking rather than defending | Opens the conversation past the surface; what was actually hurt, not just the event |
| **Progression** | Conciliatory turns, acknowledgment, signals of listening; shorter exchanges | Signals willingness; associated with decreases in anger and fear |
| **Mutual engagement** | Balanced exchange, both contributing, short turns | Both people in the conversation together; associated with decreased hurt and increased contentment |

Conversations get stuck in Exposition when the person who caused the rupture starts
defending instead of asking. The move to Elicitation requires stopping the defense and
asking: what was the actual impact? Not just what happened, but what it cost the other
person. The specific repair moment is when the person who caused harm names the exact
mismatch — not just "I'm sorry" but "I see what you needed and what I did instead."

**Repair per foundation:**

*Coupling:* The nervous system now associates shared presence with threat. This is a body
problem, not a conversation problem. Conversation before regulation will not work. Individual
regulation first. Then re-entry through low-stakes shared activity with no emotional
expectation. Then gradually increasing the weight of activities only as co-presence
re-establishes ease.

*PPR:* The repair moment requires the person who misresponded to name the exact
mismatch: what kind of response was needed, what was given instead. General apology
without specific recognition does not repair PPR because it does not demonstrate that the
accuracy problem has been identified.

*Schema:* Demonstrate that old if-then scripts still hold where they should. Create new
interactions that produce positive peaks. Then actively reminisce about the repair itself —
"remember when that went badly and we worked through it." This counterintuitively
strengthens the schema by adding evidence that the bond survives rupture. A schema that
has never been tested has no evidence of its own resilience.

## Open Questions

Two genuine gaps:

**Passive decay of relational schema:** Undo is intentional deconstruction. What happens
to schema during extended fallow periods — not deliberate dismantling, but long stretches
where neither reminiscing nor new shared experiences occur? This matters for bonds that
go through periods of forced separation (incarceration, migration, illness, structural
isolation) and then attempt to reconvene. How much persists, under what conditions, and
what rebuilding looks like are unspecified. Schema is the only one of the three foundations
that persists beyond its building activities, which makes it the only one with a meaningful
passive decay question.

**Polycule structural asymmetry:** When a three-way polycule partially dissolves, the
group-level schema disappears. If the remaining dyadic bonds were partly held up by the
group structure — if some of what looked like closeness between two specific people was
actually group-mediated — the remaining bond discovers how much was actually between
them specifically. There is community knowledge of this phenomenon but no empirical
research on it. The framework predicts it; prediction is not yet evidence.
